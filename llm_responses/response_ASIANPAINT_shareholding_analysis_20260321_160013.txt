<h3>1. Shareholding Composition Overview</h3>

<h4>1.1 Shareholding Structure — Latest Available Pattern (Q3 FY25 / December 2024)</h4>

<p>Asian Paints Limited maintains a well-defined ownership structure dominated by its founding promoter families, making it one of the most promoter-concentrated large-cap companies on Indian exchanges. The promoter group — comprising the Choksey, Dani, Vakil, and Vora families — has historically held a stable block exceeding 52%, which provides strong management continuity and long-term strategic alignment. This concentrated promoter ownership is a double-edged sword: it ensures disciplined capital allocation but also limits free float, which can create liquidity constraints for large institutional investors.</p>

<h3>2. Promoter Group Detailed Breakdown</h3>

<h4>2.1 Founding Family Holdings — Structural Analysis</h4>

<p>Asian Paints was founded in 1942 by four families — Choksey, Dani, Vakil, and Vora — and their collective holding remains the bedrock of the company's ownership structure. Unlike many Indian promoter groups, Asian Paints' promoters have demonstrated exceptional restraint in not pledging shares or diluting holdings, which is a mark of high corporate governance quality. The absence of any promoter pledge is particularly noteworthy given the company's significant capital expenditure requirements for capacity expansion.</p>

<p>The four founding families hold near-equal stakes, which is unusual in Indian business history and reflects a balanced power structure that has survived generational transitions. This parity in ownership prevents any single family from dominating strategic decisions unilaterally, which has historically contributed to the company's culture of professional management. The transition to professional CEOs (most recently Amit Syngle as MD &amp; CEO) while families retain board representation is a hallmark of governance maturity.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Zero promoter pledge</strong> across all four family groups eliminates the risk of forced selling or margin calls — a critical governance comfort for institutional investors.</li>
  <li><strong>No insider selling</strong> in recent quarters despite the stock correcting ~30-35% from its all-time highs in FY23-24, suggesting promoters view current prices as fair or undervalued relative to long-term potential.</li>
  <li><strong>Generational succession risk</strong> is a latent concern — as the founding generation ages, estate planning and inheritance-related stake transfers could introduce minor volatility, though no such events have been announced.</li>
</ul>

<h3>3. Institutional Investor Analysis</h3>

<h4>3.1 Foreign Portfolio Investor (FPI/FII) Trend — 5-Quarter Analysis</h4>

<p>FPI holdings in Asian Paints have been on a consistent downward trajectory over the past 6-8 quarters, reflecting a broader reassessment of premium consumer staples valuations in India alongside company-specific concerns. The entry of Grasim Industries' Birla Opus brand into the decorative paints segment in FY24 has materially altered the competitive landscape, prompting foreign investors to reduce exposure. This FPI selling has been a key contributor to the stock's underperformance relative to the broader Nifty 50 index.</p>
